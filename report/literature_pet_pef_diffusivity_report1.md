
# Oxygen and Water Diffusivity in PET and PEF (Room Temperature ~300 K)

## Oxygen Diffusivity in PET vs PEF

Experimental studies indicate that oxygen diffusion in PET is on the order of **10⁻¹³ m²/s at room temperature**.  
In amorphous PET at ~25 °C, the oxygen diffusion coefficient is typically reported around:

**D(O₂) ≈ 5.6 × 10⁻¹³ m²·s⁻¹** (≈5.6 × 10⁻⁹ cm²·s⁻¹).

Semi‑crystalline PET often exhibits somewhat lower diffusivity (~3.3 × 10⁻¹³ m²·s⁻¹) due to impermeable crystalline domains reducing the effective diffusion pathway.

Poly(ethylene furanoate) (PEF), the bio‑based analog of PET, shows significantly lower oxygen diffusivity. Multiple studies report that:

**O₂ diffusion in PEF is roughly 5–10× lower than in PET.**

Representative computational predictions consistent with experiments:

| Polymer | D(O₂) (cm²/s) | D(O₂) (m²/s) |
|---|---|---|
| PET | 3.24 × 10⁻⁸ | 3.24 × 10⁻¹² |
| PEF | 2.88 × 10⁻⁹ | 2.88 × 10⁻¹³ |

This reduction arises from structural differences:

- PEF contains **furan rings**, which restrict chain mobility.
- PET contains **phenyl rings**, which allow greater segmental motion.
- Reduced chain dynamics in PEF limit transient free‑volume formation, slowing penetrant diffusion.

Experimentally this translates into significantly improved barrier performance. Oxygen permeability measurements show that:

**PEF exhibits ~10–11× lower oxygen permeability than PET at ~35 °C.**

The improved barrier properties are therefore mainly attributed to suppressed molecular mobility and tighter packing in the amorphous phase.

## Water (H₂O) Diffusivity in PET vs PEF

Water diffusion in PET is also extremely slow at room temperature. Typical experimental values for amorphous PET are:

**D(H₂O) ≈ 2–3 × 10⁻¹³ m²·s⁻¹**  
(≈2.7 × 10⁻⁹ cm²·s⁻¹ at ~30 °C).

PEF again shows slower diffusion. Experimental and modeling work indicates:

**H₂O diffusivity in PEF is roughly 3–5× lower than in PET.**

Representative comparison:

| Polymer | D(H₂O) (m²/s) |
|---|---|
| PET | ~2–3 × 10⁻¹³ |
| PEF | ~0.5–1 × 10⁻¹³ |

However, an important difference between the two polymers is **water sorption**.

PEF absorbs more moisture than PET because:

- The **furan ring is more polar** than the phenyl ring.
- This increases polymer–water interactions.

Reported equilibrium water uptake:

| Polymer | Relative water uptake |
|---|---|
| PET | baseline |
| PEF | ~1.8× higher |

Because permeability is the product of diffusivity and solubility:

**P = D × S**

the higher water solubility in PEF partially offsets its lower diffusivity. As a result:

- **Water permeability of PEF is roughly ~2× lower than PET**, not 5× lower.

## Summary of Typical Diffusion Coefficients (≈300 K)

| Polymer | Penetrant | Diffusivity (m²/s) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| PET | O₂ | ~3–6 × 10⁻¹³ | Amorphous polymer |
| PEF | O₂ | ~3 × 10⁻¹⁴ – 1 × 10⁻¹³ | ~5–10× lower than PET |
| PET | H₂O | ~2–3 × 10⁻¹³ | Weakly dependent on humidity |
| PEF | H₂O | ~0.5–1 × 10⁻¹³ | Lower diffusivity but higher sorption |
